Japan: Stability and Innovation in a Turbulent Era
Abenomics has reinvigorated Japan’s spirit and demonstrated the nation’s resilience, spurring innovation and growth at home and strengthening economic partnerships globally.
Robust foundations

In January 2019, Japan passed a striking milestone: the economy notched up its longest recovery since the Second World War. The 74-month expansion was even more remarkable given that the period was marred by economic turbulence abroad and natural disasters at home, including last year’s earthquake in Hokkaido, the northernmost of Japan’s four main islands.

The Year of the Boar, as 2019 falls according to the Japanese cycle of zodiac signs, is proving to be auspicious in other ways too. In February, the EU-Japan Economic Partnership Agreement, which creates a trade zone covering about a third of the world’s GDP, came into effect. The deal demonstrates Japan’s pledge to take the global lead as a “flag-bearer for free trade”.

Japan pushed through another ambitious free-trade agreement, the Comprehensive and Progressive Agreement for Trans-Pacific Partnership (CPTPP), which covers 11 countries and more than 13% of the global economy, in 2018. Both deals belie the nation’s reputation for being closed to trade and follow through on the determination to drill through the “bedrock” of vested interests and make Japan the “easiest country in the world” for business.

Global inflows of foreign direct investment (FDI) are expected to hit their fourth annual record high this year. That puts FDI on track to meet a plan to double its 2012 value by 2020. Corporate tax rates are falling and the government has approved a 20% rate to spur jobs and investment. A corporate governance code enacted in June 2018 is increasing board accountability and business transparency.

Japan was once written off by economic pundits, notes Jesper Koll, head of WisdomTree Japan, an exchange-traded fund manager. The collapse of the asset and land bubble in the 1990s “wiped out one generation of wealth,” he says. The nation seemed snared in deflation and policy stasis. Yet, as Japan has recovered, it has grown in economic stability and managed to balance income growth and distribution, an equilibrium that is presently growing more imbalanced in some developed countries. “In terms of social cohesion, civility and public services, Japan is exemplary”, Mr Koll concludes.

Smart growth

One of the factors largely responsible for Japan’s success is its continued ability to seamlessly incorporate new technology into both business and society. The nation’s strength saw Tokyo beat London and New York to top the 2018 list of the world’s most innovative cities in an annual index compiled by 2thinknow, a consultancy. The city won by investing heavily in smart technology “to lead innovation and leadership in robotics and 3D manufacturing”, according to 2thinknow.

Japanese factories already have one of the highest densities of robots in the world and the vast service industry is now starting to automate, with national banks recently announcing a seismic shift to automation and artificial intelligence. This is a positive sign of resilience in a country faced with a rapidly aging population. As robots and AI become more sophisticated, they will gradually replace low-skilled office and service work, explains Yoko Takeda of Mitsubishi Research Institute, a Japanese think tank. She predicts that Japan may have a small labour surplus by 2030.

 

The notion that robots and AI will help lead economic growth and replace repetitive tasks is central to the Future Investment Strategy 2018, the government’s blueprint for growth. Domestically, it is aiming for the fifth stage of human evolution. Society 5.0, as it is called, will use technology to create a society “where the needs of each individual are met”. The government has also designated 10 areas of the country for looser regulations and credits to bolster economic growth. It is funding 37 universities through its Top Global University Japan programme, and 13 departments via its World Premier International Research Center Initiative to boost the internationalisation of higher education. Many of these universities are at the forefront of scientific collaboration with foreign institutions, including the Université de Strasbourg in France, the Karlsruhe Institute of Technology (KIT) in Germany and the University of California Los Angeles (UCLA), among many others.

The International Institute for Integrative Sleep Medicine at the University of Tsukuba, for example, has discovered a gene that regulates sleep. Elsewhere, researchers from the Kavli Institute for the Physics and Mathematics of the Universe at Tokyo University won the 2018 Breakthrough Prize in Fundamental Physics for developing detailed maps of the early universe.

Last year, the Council for Science, Technology and Innovation, the government’s science advisory body, announced a 7% rise in spending on science and technology, reversing years of stagnant funding. The government is also mulling another 100 billion to pay for exploratory research. Japan is on track to hit its goal of spending 1% of GDP on science and technology by 2020, up from 0.65% in 2015.

Women power, grey power

Female empowerment is improving in Japan. The country moved up four places in the latest (2018) Global Gender Gap Index (it scores high in health and education but low in politics). The Japanese female labour force participation rate rose from 46.2 percent in 2012 to 51.3 percent in 2017, an historic change. Women such as Kathy Matsui, who coined the phrase “womenomics”, are becoming increasingly active in business and civil society.

Female entrepreneurs such as Emi Takemura, co-founder of Peatix, a successful Tokyo-based online ticketing platform, and Akiko Naka, who took her company Wantedly, a professional social networking service, public on the Tokyo Stock Exchange in 2017, are challenging the globally male-dominated business world. In the US, for example, as of May 2018, less than 5% of Fortune 500 companies had female CEOs. Still in her mid-thirties, Naka is one of the youngest women ever to run a Japanese-listed company.

Most of the world’s developed economies are only just beginning to grapple with the economic implications of their rapidly aging societies—but Japan has a head start in finding solutions. One in four Japanese is over 65; by 2035 it will be one in three, predicts the government. That makes it the world’s laboratory for developing innovative products and services for older people. Japan is showing the world the benefits of its ageing society by incorporating millions of over 65s into its economy, building one of the world’s more effective healthcare systems, and creating innovative products and services for the elderly.

Trading company Mitsui, for example, has developed a prototype of a robot suit, with Panasonic, that helps the elderly with their mobility. Fujitsu, an electronics firm, has sold 20 million of its “Raku Raku” mobile phones, which have larger buttons and simplified functions that older people can use more easily. Separately, a venture firm affiliated with the University of Tokyo has launched a sensor system that measures heart rate, body temperature, respiration, pulse and other vitals, which can be monitored using a remote Internet connection. Many more companies, from Nitto to Sony, to Triple W Japan, have made an economic and societal positive out of Japan’s demographic challenges.

Cohesion, resilience

Economic and technological change can be disruptive, but Japan’s social cohesion has allowed it to ride out previous waves of creative destruction. “Japan seems to have bottom-up sources of resilience—strong network ties within communities,” argues Tobias Harris, vice president of Teneo Intelligence, a risk consultancy.

Japan’s economic and social resilience, along with its ability to harness technology to find solutions to some of the world’s most pressing challenges, has made the country a beacon of stability during a globally turbulent time. This inner strength is reflected in its ability to withstand and grow from great challenges. Eight years ago, for example, a tsunami destroyed infrastructure worth 10% of Japan’s GDP. The 2011 Great East Japan earthquake was seen by some as the “final nail in Japan’s economic coffin”, recalls author David Pilling. Yet, it may instead come to be recorded as a turning point, yet another example of Japan’s resilience and its history of “bending adversity”.
